# SaaS Growth Playbook

## What This Skill Does

This skill turns an early-stage SaaS growth question into a concrete operating plan.

Use it to:
- validate a SaaS idea before building too much
- identify the current growth bottleneck
- choose the next-best channel instead of spreading effort everywhere
- sequence outbound, inbound, paid, and SEO/GEO in the right order
- produce a clear 30-day and 90-day plan with experiments and decision thresholds

This is a plan-first skill. By default, you create the growth plan before proposing any execution workflow.

---

## When to Use

Invoke this skill when the user asks things like:
- "Help me grow my SaaS"
- "Build a GTM plan for this product"
- "What channel should I use first?"
- "Turn this offer into a 90-day growth plan"
- "How do I get my first SaaS customers?"
- "Should I build more or sell more?"

This skill is best for:
- pre-launch SaaS founders
- SaaS products with weak or inconsistent traction
- founder-led sales and founder-led GTM
- products deciding whether to focus on outbound, inbound, paid, or SEO next

Do not treat this skill as permission to execute outreach, ads, or content automatically unless the user separately asks for execution.

## Inputs

Collect or infer these inputs before planning:

| Input | What to capture |
|---|---|
| Product | What the SaaS does in one sentence |
| Problem | The painful problem being solved |
| ICP | Ideal buyer, company type, team size, and urgency |
| Stage | Idea only, pre-launch, early traction, or scaling |
| Traction | Users, calls booked, trials, revenue, retention, and proof |
| Budget | Amount available for growth experiments this month |
| Founder constraints | Time, skill gaps, ability to do sales, and channel preferences |
| Existing assets | Landing page, demo, content, list, social following, customer proof, testimonials |
| Sales motion | Self-serve, sales-assisted, or enterprise-style |

If inputs are missing, make the smallest reasonable assumptions and state them explicitly in the plan.

## Core Operating Model

Default to this sequence unless evidence clearly justifies a different order:

1. Validate the problem and offer before building more product
2. Use outbound first to get direct signal from the market
3. Layer inbound once messaging starts working
4. Add paid only after you have proof that the offer converts
5. Add SEO / GEO as a compounding channel after the offer and messaging are clearer

This skill optimizes for:
- revenue signal over product polish
- real buyer conversations over abstract market research
- one bottleneck at a time
- clear experiments over generic marketing advice

## Offer Validation Rules

Follow these rules before recommending heavy build work:

### 1. Validate the problem first

The founder should be able to describe:
- who has the problem
- why the problem is expensive or urgent
- what existing workaround the buyer uses today
- what outcome the buyer would pay for

If that is fuzzy, recommend customer conversations before product work.

### 2. Sell before polishing

Prefer activities that create market signal:
- book calls with likely buyers
- test positioning on cold outreach
- collect reactions to a landing page or offer deck
- close manual or concierge-style pilots

Do not recommend building large features with no validation signal.

### 3. Only build when market pull is visible

Signs of real signal include:
- qualified buyers agreeing to calls
- repeated pain language across multiple conversations
- pilots, LOIs, paid trials, or early revenue
- prospects asking for implementation details or security / procurement steps

If the founder has no signal, bias toward outbound and problem discovery.

### 4. Prioritize the highest-intent path

Recommend the channel most likely to create learning and revenue in the next 30 days, not the channel that sounds most scalable later.

## Channel Sequencing

### 1. Outbound — Start Here

Use outbound first unless the user already has strong inbound traction.

Best for:
- zero to first calls
- weak traction
- unclear messaging
- founder-led selling

Target accounts with visible buying signals:
- hiring for roles your product replaces or augments
- posting about painful workflows
- active growth initiatives or operational bottlenecks
- manual processes with obvious labor cost
- founder-led companies where the pain is easy to map to revenue or cost

Outbound plan should include:
- ICP shortlist
- 2 to 3 message angles
- one simple offer
- target number of conversations
- success threshold for message-market fit

Avoid broad spray-and-pray lists. Use narrow, high-intent targeting.

### 2. Inbound — Add Once Outbound Messaging Works

Once outbound reveals which pain, promise, and buyer language resonate, use inbound to amplify that signal.

Recommended inbound building blocks:
- founder content around one painful wedge
- case studies and proof posts
- lead magnets tied to the buyer's problem
- landing pages focused on one ICP and one outcome
- reviewable demos, teardown content, or comparisons

Inbound should not become a random content treadmill. Every piece should map to:
- one ICP
- one bottleneck
- one CTA

### 3. Paid Ads — Only After Conversion Proof

Paid should come after:
- offer clarity
- working landing page
- evidence that demos or trials convert
- confidence in ICP and message

If those are missing, paid usually burns money faster than it creates signal.

Use paid only when the user has:
- budget to absorb testing
- a clear CTA
- baseline conversion data
- a reason to believe more traffic will help

### 4. SEO / GEO — Build for Compounding Return

SEO / GEO is slow but durable. Recommend it after the product, ICP, and messaging are more stable.

Good SEO / GEO starting points:
- category pages and comparison pages
- pain-point content matched to buyer search intent
- use-case pages for ICP segments
- customer proof and case studies
- founder expertise content that answers high-intent questions

Treat this as a weekly compounding system, not the first growth lever.

## Demo Routing Heuristics

Use a simple routing recommendation in the plan:

| Lead Type | Recommended Demo Motion |
|---|---|
| Unclear ICP or low-intent cold lead | Short group demo, founder webinar, or qualification call |
| Small team with moderate fit | Lightweight live demo |
| High-fit qualified team | 1:1 founder-led demo |
| Warm lead with strong urgency | Direct booking to personalized demo |

General rule:
- lower intent → lower-touch routing
- higher fit and urgency → higher-touch routing

Do not recommend a full bespoke demo for every lead if qualification is still weak.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Diagnose the Current Stage

Classify the company into one of these states:
- **Pre-signal**: no customers, little proof, still validating the problem
- **Signal found**: repeated buyer pain and some call traction
- **Early customers**: first users or revenue, but growth is not repeatable
- **Scaling a wedge**: one channel or motion is working and needs amplification

Then identify the single biggest bottleneck:
- unclear ICP
- weak positioning
- too few conversations
- poor demo conversion
- weak retention or proof
- too many channels with too little depth

### Step 2 — Rewrite the Offer Clearly

Produce:
- one-sentence positioning
- target buyer
- painful trigger event
- promised outcome
- reason to believe

If the offer is vague, do not move to channel expansion yet.

### Step 3 — Choose the Next-Best Channel

Use these defaults:
- no traction → outbound
- some traction but unclear message → outbound + message refinement
- working message but low awareness → inbound
- stable funnel and budget → selective paid
- stable narrative and proof → SEO / GEO

Recommend one primary channel and one secondary support channel at most.

### Step 4 — Design the 30-Day Plan

The first 30 days should focus on signal, not scale.

Include:
- one primary goal
- 3 to 5 experiments
- one owner per experiment
- expected signal from each experiment
- decision threshold to continue, revise, or stop

Good 30-day goals:
- book first 10 qualified calls
- validate one ICP and one message
- close first pilot
- improve demo-to-trial conversion

### Step 5 — Design the 90-Day Plan

Organize around three phases:

| Phase | Focus |
|---|---|
| Days 1-30 | Get signal |
| Days 31-60 | Convert signal into customers |
| Days 61-90 | Scale what already works |

For each phase, define:
- objective
- primary channel
- key experiments
- success criteria

### Step 6 — Add Experiment Logic

Every experiment must state:
- hypothesis
- audience
- channel
- offer / CTA
- effort level
- expected signal
- threshold for success or failure

Examples:
- 50 outbound messages to a narrow ICP segment
- 3 founder posts testing 3 message angles
- one landing page rewrite with a clearer CTA
- one lead magnet tied to a recurring buyer pain

### Step 7 — Recommend What Not to Do

Explicitly call out distractions to avoid, such as:
- building features before buyer proof
- running paid with no conversion baseline
- publishing content for broad awareness when ICP is still unclear
- trying all four channels at once

## Required Output Format

When using this skill, respond in this structure:

### 1. Positioning
- one-sentence product positioning

### 2. Current Bottleneck
- the single biggest constraint on growth right now

### 3. Next-Best Channel
- primary channel
- why this is the right next move
- what not to prioritize yet

### 4. 30-Day Plan
- 3 to 5 concrete experiments
- owner, effort, expected signal, and threshold for each

### 5. 90-Day Plan
- days 1-30
- days 31-60
- days 61-90

### 6. Demo Routing Recommendation
- how to route low-, medium-, and high-intent leads

### 7. Validation Checklist
- proof still needed before scaling

### 8. Assumptions
- any assumptions made due to missing information

Keep the response concrete, prioritized, and stage-aware.

## Pitfalls

Watch for these mistakes:
- recommending paid or SEO too early
- overvaluing product polish when the offer is still unproven
- giving channel advice without naming the current bottleneck
- telling the founder to "do content" without a wedge, ICP, and CTA
- treating all leads as equally worthy of a high-touch demo
- proposing too many simultaneous experiments
- giving generic startup advice instead of a stage-specific plan

If the user has no traction, the answer should feel more like a validation plan than a scaling plan.

## Verification

The skill worked if the final answer:
- identifies the company stage correctly
- names one clear bottleneck
- chooses a primary channel in the right sequence
- includes a concrete 30-day and 90-day plan
- contains explicit experiments and decision thresholds
- recommends buyer conversations and selling early when proof is weak
- does not imply automatic execution unless the user separately asked for it

If any of those are missing, revise the plan before presenting it.
